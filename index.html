Understanding
the
Mechanics
behind
Nicknames
and
Namecalling

Nicknames are a part of everyday life. We use them to connect with friends and family, show friendliness to our coworkers, and signal closeness. Beyond interactions with other people, we also frequently modify the names of events, places, courses, and other aspects of our lives. Wordplay, as it's known, presents itself commonly in games and puzzles, conversational humor, and broad-based media. Much of it feels clever and funny because it depends on a recognizable connection between the original form and the modified one. When people understand the reasoning behind these modifications, wordplay can create a sense of shared knowledge. Several linguistic aspects, including substitution, minimal pairs, and periodic preservation make for effective puns, nicknames, and name calling.

In its simplest form, substitution refers to replacing a word, or part of a word, with another sound or linguistic element. Many nicknames and puns are created using different forms of substitution, like onset (Brook → Crook), vowel (Ben → Bun), coda (Matt → Mad), blend/portmanteau (APMA 0360 → CRAPMA 3-Shitty), or affixation substitution (Shell → Shelly).1 Generally, the more layered the substitution is, such as combining multiple substitution types, the more satisfying the pun feels. For example, APMA 0360 → CRAPMA 3-Shitty works through multiple overlapping and rhyming effects, while Ben → Bun uses just one vowel change.

Many forms of substitution are also minimal pairs–words that are identical in all sounds but one (e.g. got vs get).2 Minimal pairs are the foundation of creating worthwhile nicknames, and having an understanding of how different pairs interact with each other can help create more complex forms. For example, APMA 1200 can be broken into several phoneme pairs: APMA: APP - muh, 1200: TWELV HUN-drid, and can be altered to exhibit several qualities of the class: APMA 1200 → CRAPMA Twelve-bun-dread. The modification makes use of different minimal pairs (apma → crapma, hun → bun, drid → dread) while keeping an identifying piece of the original form (twelve). Prosody preservation maintains the rhythm and emotion behind original speech and is necessary for the audience to understand the pun. For example, when a word has a distinctive stress pattern, an effective modification would attempt to preserve that prosody. For example, Beyoncé → Beyon-slay works because it keeps the rhythm and stress pattern of the original name intact. By contrast, Beyoncé → Queen-oncé feels less natural. Although Bey and Queen are both one syllable, they are not prosodically equivalent. Queen ends with an /n/ sound, making the syllable feel closed and heavier: queen = /kwi:n/. Bey, By contrast, is open and lighter: bey = /beɪ/.

Examples

To better understand how phonological substitution works in real contexts, we have compiled a number of nickname and wordplay examples from three primary sources: historical and literary texts, social media platforms, and personal interactions. This range will let us capture both long-standing traditions of wordplay and also more contemporary or rapidly evolving forms.

From historical and literary sources, we see that playful renaming has deep roots. For example, William Shakespeare often used puns based on sound similarity, often relying on rhyming and minimal pair structures.3 One well-known instance comes from Romeo and Juliet, where Mercutio puns on the word “grave.” As he dies, he says “ask for me tomorrow, and you shall find me a grave man.” Grave here means “serious” and “dead/buried.” The humor comes from the phonological identity of the words paired with two different meanings. Just a fun note – Gnomeo and Juliet, the animated adaptation of Romeo and Juliet with gnomes, is another example of play on words. The name Romeo is transformed into Gnomeo through onset substitution. This pun keeps the original phonological structure while introducing a new semantic element, making the reference immediately recognizable and humorous.

Moreover, in political contexts, nicknames have long been used to convey critique or satire. For example, figures like Richard Nixon were labeled with modified names like “Tricky Dick,” which uses rhyming and affixation to convey negative meaning while remaining memorable.

Social media provides a more dynamic and creative dataset. Platforms like Tiktok, Reddit, and Twitter (X) are especially rich in nickname formation. Users often create names like “Sleepy Joe” for Joe Biden or “Florida Man,” which rely on rhythm, repetition, and cultural context. Rhyming constructions like “Slick Rick” or “Messy Jessie” are also common, showing how prosodic patterns enhance memorability. These examples tend to spread quickly because they are easy to recognize and replicate.

Personal and in-group examples show another layer of usage. Within friend groups or classrooms, nicknames often rely on shared experiences or inside jokes. As seen in Part 1, APMA 1200 becoming “CRAPMA Twelve-bun-dread” shows multiple substitution types, including blending (APMA → CRAPMA), vowel substitution (hun → bun), and rhyming (drid → dread). Similarly, names like “Snack Zack” or “Lazy Daisy,” combine rhyming with semantic labeling, reinforcing both sound similarity and descriptive meaning.

Each example in our dataset was annotated based on four criteria: the original and modified form, the type of substitution used, the semantic meaning introduced, and the social context. This allowed us to compare how different substitution strategies function across settings. Several patterns emerged from this analysis. First, simpler substitutions, especially onset changes and rhyming, appear most frequently. These forms are easy to create and recognize, making them effective in fast-paced environments like social media. Second, more complex forms, like blends and multi-layered substitutions, are less common but often perceived as more creative or humorous. These tend to appear in closer social groups where shared knowledge supports more elaborate wordplay.

Finally, context plays an important role in determining how a nickname is interpreted. The same phonological structure can feel affectionate, humorous, or insulting depending on who is using it and why. This highlights that while phonological patterns provide the structure for nickname formation, meaning is ultimately shaped by social use.

Overall, this dataset demonstrates that nickname formation is not random, but follows recognizable patterns that balance phonological similarity, semantic meaning, and social context. These findings provide the empirical foundation for both our analysis and the design of our interactive nickname generator.

Pragmatics
and
Sociolinguistics

Phonological structure explains how nicknames like “Crook” from “Brook” are constructed, but it does not explain exactly why they land. Their success depends on social context: shared knowledge, relationships between speakers, and the direction of the critique.

The first of the criteria is shared knowledge the audience already knows what is being referenced. The concept of common ground captures this: speakers rely on information they assume is shared.4 “Crook” only works if the listener knows who Brook is and understands the implication or context of the nickname. Without it, the form does not read as clever, it just reads like a different word. This pattern holds across the examples provided earlier. In-group examples like “CRAPMA 3-Shitty” depend on specific shared class experiences, while broader forms like “Tricky Dick” rely on widely available cultural knowledge.

The second of the success criteria is the relationship between the speakers and understanding that not everyone is privy to use these forms. Playful renaming signals an in-group. Using a nickname implies a closeness and familiarity with both the phrase and the context, which means the same phrase can shift meaning depending on who uses it. For example, if a close friend uses the nickname, it would read as affiliative. However, if an outsider uses the same phrase, it could feel presumptuous or cause confusion. In both situations, the phonological structure stays the same, but the interpretation does not. Therefore, social position determines how the nickname is received.

The third and final success criteria of these nicknames is the direction of the critique because many of these forms carry a judgment, which brings in power. Dysphemism describes the use of loaded substitutions. When the course “APMA 0360” is renamed to “CRAPMA 3-Shitty,” the critique is directed upward at the professor and the course material, which makes it feel collective and justified.5 Political nicknames like “Sleepy Joe” operate similarly, as critique is packaged into short, repeatable forms. But when the same kind of renaming is directed toward someone with less social power, or used in contexts where the target is not present, it can lose some of its playful quality and take on a more negative tone. The form stays the same, but the social meaning shifts.

These forms are both playful and offensive, which is where face theory comes in.6 A modified name can affect both a positive face, wanting to be liked, and a negative face, wanting control over how you are represented.7 What keeps it from going too far is indirection. Because the critique is implied rather than stated outright and the nickname is similar to the original name, it softens it. But if that balance is missing and if there is no shared understanding or no relationship to support it, the same nickname can easily feel like an insult.

Overall, playful renaming works both because it sounds right, and because it is shaped by social context: it depends on shared knowledge, relational context, and power. These constraints determine whether a form is successful or not.

Using
Our
Research
to
Make
a
Nicknaming
Tool

As a continued exploration of nicknaming, we built a digital Nicknamer. It is programmed with the linguistic constraints of playful renaming by prioritizing phonological preservation. At its core, the logic utilizes the CMU Pronouncing Dictionary to identify rhyme tails, the sequence of phonemes from the primary stressed vowel to the end of the word. By isolating these tails, the Nicknamer ensures that the generated nickname retains the prosodic skeleton of a target syllable, satisfying the structural requirement that a nickname must "sound right" to be recognizable.

In instances where a direct rhyme is unavailable, the logic shifts to nuclei matching. Here, the algorithm matches the target's vowel phonemes (e.g., /IY1/ in "Brook") regardless of the surrounding consonants. This computational flexibility mirrors the natural linguistic tendency toward assonance in wordplay, where the "vibe" of a sound is preserved even when the strict rhyme is broken, sometimes called near rhyming. By allowing users to override these sounds via the dropdown menu, the tool acknowledges that phonetic interpretation is subjective and context-dependent.

Ultimately, what the Nicknamer lacks is the human intelligence/pragmatic competence to provide semantically distinct substitutes. While it succeeds at finding nicknames that preserve phonetical similarity, this is only one piece of the puzzle. Bear this in mind when using the Nicknamer. It is programmed to provide the structural "how" through rhyme and meter, but it is blind to the common ground and power dynamics that constitute the "why". If the results may provide the "dysphemistic" substitutions that define in-group social bonding, it is purely by chance!
